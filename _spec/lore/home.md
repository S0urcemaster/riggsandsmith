# Home

## Definition

Home is the private living space in which John Riggs conducts the hidden part of his life as Lucky Smith.

It is not an elegant hacker lair.

It is a slightly messy combined work room, bedroom, and living space shaped by technical obsession, limited money, and repeated improvised use.

## Spatial Character

The room should feel heavily used, dense with equipment, and governed by a kind of chaotic logic.

It is not clean in a minimalist sense, but it is also not random.

The disorder has structure.

Things are where Riggs expects them to be.

The room should suggest a person who keeps too many active systems in too little space and still knows how the whole arrangement works.

## Equipment Density

Home contains a large amount of visible technology.

Nearly every place where Riggs spends time has some kind of screen or computing surface.

This includes at minimum:

- the bed area
- the combined work and eating table
- the rest and gaming spot

The main work table is the densest operating point in the room.

It carries the rig work area and uses three monitors.

The room should therefore read less like a normal apartment and more like an ordinary life that has been overtaken by technical accumulation.

## Tone Details

Home should communicate:

- low-budget technical intensity
- practical improvisation
- familiar daily life under pressure
- privacy mixed with risk
- obsession without theatrical perfection

One useful detail is a picture frame with no picture inside it.

It suggests absence, interruption, or a life detail that has been stripped away without turning the room into melodrama.

## World Function

Home matters because it makes the illegal work concrete.

Riggs does not disappear into an abstract menu.

He returns to a cramped personal environment where he sleeps, eats, works, plays, and runs forbidden machinery.

This gives the product a stronger sense of double life, routine, and personal exposure.

## Boundary Rule

The lore may define Home more richly than the first playable slice visibly renders it.

For example, the world truth may include more screens, furniture detail, clutter logic, and environmental traces than are shown in an early implementation.

The product specification must decide which parts of Home are actually visible, interactive, or required for the current build.
